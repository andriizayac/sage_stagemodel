Stage-structured populations using IDE

Payton Lyons, Andrii Zaiats, Maisha Maliha, Rongsong Liu, Trevor Caughlin
Fall 2021

  1. Background.

Ecosystem degradation in the Intermountain Western US has been an increasing trend over the last century. Intensifying wildfires, biological invasions, and land use are among the major agents driving the degradation of local ecosystems, including plant and animal populations. Land managers and stakeholds across the Western US rely on management techniques that can help in the recovery of natrual ecosystem states, but the spatial extent of the disturbance exceeds the resources that are at the disposal of land managers. Mathematical models that mechanistically describe the dynamics of plant populations can be a powerful tool to guide management interventions by predicting population dynamics, including natural rates of recovery, seed dispersal from remnant populations, demographic bottlenecks in recovery, natural and alternative stable states. Analytical insights, combined with numerical simulations of such mechanistic models can also help in generating management hypotheses and intervention scenrarios that can further improve effective managemnet of the disturbed areas.

  1. Objectives.

  • Construct a mathematical stage-structured population model of big sagebrush.

  • Quantify the efficacy of common intervention strategies on rates of population recovery, including aerial seeding, planting design and density.

  • Quantify the sensitivity of population recovery to variations in intrinsic growth and dispersal parameters.

  • Derive the existence of positive stable states in heterogenous environments indicative of population resilience to disturbance agents.

  1. Integro-Difference Equations for structured populations.

Modeling populations of long-living, perennial plants requires approaches that differentiate plants in a population according their size or age at any given time (Caswell 2000). A simplified approach to describing plant size structure is a framework where we can break down population life cycle into two stages: mature and juvenile individuals. The fundamental difference between these two stages is that mature plants are sessile and reproduce by creating new recruits in the vicinity of parent plants, while juvenile individuals do not reproduce, and their discritbution and esteblishement depend on the location and abundance of mature plants. Mathematically, this structure could be described by the following piece-wise function:

\[ \begin{align} H_{t+1} = \begin{cases} H_t & \quad \text{if} \, t > \tau \\ S_t & \quad \text{if} \, 0 \geq t < \tau \end{cases} \end{align} \]

where $H_{t+1}$ is the population at the next time step, $H_t$ and $S_t$ describe the mature and juvenile individuals in the population respectively. This is modeled as a piecewise function to accurately meet the condition about how the plants grow differently at different time periods in it’s lifespan. Becuase the amount of seeds plants produce per year is proportional to the state and amount of mature indiviudlas, the number of juvenile plants produced in a population is a function of mature individuals too. We can assume each adult produces $\sigma$ seeds each time step and all plants are located in a single patch. The stage-structured model then can be described recursively, assuming the initial state $H_{t = 0} > 0$ :

\[ \begin{align} H_{t+1} = \begin{cases} H_{t} = f(H_t, S_t) \\ S_{t} = g(H_t) \end{cases} \end{align} \]

where f is a function modeling monotinic growth of mature, established, plants, and g is the function describing the influx of new individuals into the population H. The addition of new plants into the popoulation can follow an exponential relationship when the population is under low density, but is expected to decline as population matures following contest competition among individual plants. Under contest competition, larger plants are expected to disproportionally obtain resources from the environment compared to smaller plants, which leads recruitment function g to approach zero as population matures. Assuming the growth of established plants follows a monotinic function, e.g. Beverton-Holt growth model, our system can be described in the following way:

\[ \begin{align} f(H_t, S_t) &= H_t \frac{R}{1 + H_t/k} + \alpha \bigg(1 - \frac{H_t}{(r - 1)k} \bigg) S_t \\ g({H_t}) &= \sigma H_t \end{align} \]

where the first term in Eq. 3 models the growth (increase in biomass) of established individuals and the second terms describes density-dependent pattern of recruitment. Eq. 4 describes the production of new seed that can either die or establish as new individuals.

We can futher modify our system by adding a spatially explicit term that describes the distribution of juvenile plants, $S_t$, around the mature plants, $H_{t, x, y}$, using a dispersal kernel. A dispersal kernel probabilistically models the spatial distribution of juvenile plants aroud each location x, y where a mature plant is located.

\[ \begin{align} K(x,y) = \frac{1}{ \sqrt{4 \pi D}} \exp{\bigg(-\frac{x^2 - y^2}{4D} \bigg)} \\ \end{align} \]

where $x$ and $y$ indicate the location of plants on a spatial grid.

We can visualize a dispersal kernel in a single dimension, x, under a range of dispersal parameter, D, that determines how far juvenile plants are likely to establish from a parent plant located at x = 0.

Applying the dispersal kernel to the juvenile stage only we get the number of juvenile plants in any given location $x, y$ that accounts for the dispersal of plants from the neighboring patches.

\[ \begin{align} S_{x,y,t+1} = \iint \big[ K(x-x^*,y-y^*) \sigma H_{x^*,y^*, t} \big]dx^* dy^* \end{align} \]

where $x^*, y^*$ indicate the neighboring locations to $x, y$.

Putting it all together and simulating population trajectories over time using the update rules described by Eq. 1 and 2, we observe the growth of mature individuals and dispersal over time. Here, the initial conditions $H_0$, represent a scenario where plants are planted as a single, narrow strip through the spatial domain.
